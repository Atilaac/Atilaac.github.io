abstract: "In the low-frequency regime, ≈1 THz, glasses show an anomalous excess in their vibrational density of states called the boson peak (BP). The origin of BP has been a subject of debate since its first discovery a few decades ago. Although BP has been the focus of numerous studies, no conclusive answers have been found about its origins, which remained elusive to date. Here, we present results based on molecular dynamics of several binary and ternary silicate glasses with different network intermediates and modifier oxides. The vibrational density of states and the BP are reported for all the studied glasses. Their correlation with the elastic constant C44, structural, and dynamical properties are extensively discussed in terms of Voronoi atomic volume and the vibrational mean square displacement of Q4 species specifically. We also question the classical classification of alkali oxides as modifiers, and we suggest that Li2O plays the role of pseudo-intermediate oxide in lithium silicate glasses. This claim is supported by the effect of Li on various vibrational modes, and this effect differs from the other alkali metals. Furthermore, we demonstrate a correlation between the BP intensities and both the Voronoi volume of the Q4 and Q3 units and vibrational mean square displacements."
lay_summary: "Glasses have a quirky property at very low vibration frequencies: they absorb and scatter sound waves in ways that normal crystalline solids do not, a phenomenon known as the 'boson peak.' Scientists have long debated why this happens, and this study used computer simulations called molecular dynamics to investigate the boson peak across several different silicate glasses, the kind of materials found in windows, fiber optics, and many industrial applications. The researchers found that the boson peak is closely linked to how much space individual atoms occupy within the glass structure and how much those atoms wiggle around, offering new clues about its origins. They also made a surprising finding about lithium, showing that it behaves differently from other alkali metals like sodium and potassium, acting more like a structural building block in the glass network rather than simply breaking it apart. These insights could help scientists better design glass materials with tailored properties for technology and engineering applications."
