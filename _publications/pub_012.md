abstract: "Solute segregation at low-angle grain boundaries (LAGBs) critically affects the microstructure and mechanical properties of magnesium (Mg) alloys. In modern alloys containing multiple substitutional elements, understanding solute-solute interactions at microstructural defects becomes essential for alloy design. This study investigates the co-segregation mechanisms of calcium (Ca), zinc (Zn), and aluminum (Al) at a LAGB in a dilute AZX010 Mg alloy by combining atomic-scale experimental and modeling techniques. Three-dimensional atom probe tomography (3D-APT) revealed significant segregation of Ca, Zn, and Al at the LAGB, with Ca forming linear segregation patterns along dislocation arrays characteristic of the LAGB. Clustering analysis showed increased Ca-Ca pairs at the boundary, indicating synergistic solute interactions. Atomistic simulations and elastic dipole calculations demonstrated that larger Ca atoms prefer tensile regions around dislocations, while smaller Zn and Al atoms favor compressive areas. These simulations also found that Ca-Ca co-segregation near dislocation cores is energetically more favorable than other solute pairings, explaining the enhanced Ca clustering observed experimentally. Thermodynamic modeling incorporating calculated segregation energies and solute-solute interactions accurately predicted solute concentrations at the LAGB, aligning with experimental data. The findings emphasize the importance of solute interactions at dislocation cores in Mg alloys, offering insights for improving mechanical performance through targeted alloying and grain boundary engineering."
lay_summary: "Researchers studied how different metal atoms arrange themselves at the boundaries between tiny crystal grains in magnesium alloys, which are lightweight materials used in cars and aircraft. Using a powerful technique that maps individual atoms in three dimensions, they found that calcium, zinc, and aluminum atoms all cluster at these grain boundaries, with calcium atoms forming distinctive line-like patterns that follow internal defects in the material called dislocations. Computer simulations revealed why this happens: larger calcium atoms are drawn to stretched regions around dislocations while smaller zinc and aluminum atoms prefer compressed regions, and calcium atoms also have a strong tendency to pair up with each other near these defects. This atomic-level understanding matters because the way atoms arrange themselves at grain boundaries has a big influence on how strong and durable a material is. These findings could help engineers design better magnesium alloys by strategically choosing which elements to add, ultimately leading to stronger and lighter materials for use in transportation and other industries."
