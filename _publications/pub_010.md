abstract: "Glasses are typically isotropic, but permanent or transient anisotropy can be induced by tensile strain. We use persistent homology (PH) analysis to explore the topological effects of such anisotropy in SiO2single bondNa2O glass ensembles with variable alkali concentration as a function of frozen-in anisotropic strain. Statistical information was extracted on ring (H1) and cavity (H2) features, whereby anisotropic stretching was found to induce strong variations in the ring topology of the –O-Si-O- backbone, but only minor changes in the overall cavity statistics or in the Na distribution (the Na distribution was found to be governed by H2 persistence). Tensile fracture led to total recovery of network topology relative to the pristine (isotropic) glass. In revealing such reactions, PH and persistence similarity analysis may help to differentiate purely entropic orientation and irreversible structural alterations leading to glass anisotropy."
lay_summary: "Researchers studied how stretching glass changes its internal structure at the atomic level, using a mathematical technique called persistent homology to map out the hidden geometric patterns within the glass. They found that when glass is stretched, the ring-shaped arrangements of silicon and oxygen atoms that form the backbone of the material change significantly, while other structural features, like small hollow cavities and the distribution of sodium atoms, remain largely unaffected. Interestingly, when the glass was stretched until it broke, the atomic structure actually returned to its original arrangement. These findings matter because understanding how glass structure responds to stress could help scientists design stronger, more resilient glass materials and better predict how and why glass fails under pressure."
