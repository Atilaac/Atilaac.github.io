abstract: "This study advances our understanding of how chemical binding and solute distribution impact grain boundary segregation behavior and subsequent annealing texture modification in lean Mg-X-Zn alloys (X = RE or Ca). Notably, differences in Ca and Gd solute behavior at grain boundaries were revealed, where Ca exhibited stronger binding to vacancy sites than Gd, resulting in elevated Ca segregation and an RD-TD-type texture. The introduction of Zn showed significant synergistic effects on solute clustering, with Gd-Zn pairs forming more favorably than Ca-Zn pairs, leading to a strong synergy between Zn and Gd. This promoted their co-segregation and high concentration at the grain boundary, generating a unique TD-spread texture. In contrast, weaker binding in Ca-Zn pairs did not affect Ca segregation but influenced Zn segregation, which underscores the importance of solute binding behavior in alloy design concepts. Additionally, the combined atomic-scale experiments and ab initio predictions provide strong evidence that selective texture development in Mg alloys is tied to heterogeneous solute-boundary interactions, where the sensitivity of the binding energy to volumetric strain affects solute segregation at grain boundaries, resulting in varying grain boundary mobilities and specific texture component growth. It also emphasizes that solute behavior in clustering and segregation is influenced not only by atomic size but also by chemical binding strength with vacancies or co-added Zn."
lay_summary: "Researchers investigated how tiny amounts of added elements, specifically calcium, gadolinium, and zinc, behave inside magnesium metal alloys at the atomic scale. They found that calcium binds more strongly to defects in the metal's structure called vacancies, causing it to concentrate at the boundaries between metal grains and produce one type of internal pattern, while gadolinium pairs up strongly with zinc and accumulates at grain boundaries together, creating a distinctly different pattern. These differences in atomic-level behavior turn out to control how the metal's internal crystal structure, called texture, develops during heat treatment, which in turn affects the material's overall strength and how easily it can be shaped. The findings matter because magnesium alloys are lightweight and attractive for use in cars and aerospace applications, but improving their workability has been a challenge. By revealing that both the size of added atoms and their chemical bonding tendencies determine alloy behavior, this research gives engineers a clearer guide for designing better, more formable magnesium alloys."
